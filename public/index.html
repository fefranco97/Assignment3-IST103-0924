Volleyball is a dynamic team sport where two teams of six players compete to score points by hitting a ball over a net into the opponent's court. It requires agility, teamwork, and strategy, with key actions like serving, setting, spiking, and blocking. Played indoors or on sand, it's enjoyed worldwide for both recreational and competitive purposes.

Benefits of Volleyball

Personal Experience

When I was in college, I was at the beginning of a depression due to several personal issues. One day, I saw a poster inviting students to try out for the college volleyball team. I invited two classmates to join me, but in the end, I went alone. I passed the tryouts and made it onto the C team, which was the third reserve team.

I started attending practice every day, and through that, I made true friendships and formed strong bonds with my teammates. This helped me immensely in overcoming the depression I was slipping into. It gave me a greater purpose, which was to improve enough to make it onto the main team.

After three long years of hours and hours of training, I achieved my goal: I made it to the main team as a backup setter. Although I wasn’t a starter yet, I was traveling with the team to tournaments, and it felt like only a matter of time before I became a regular starter.

Then, during a quarterfinal game, I was subbed in, and we managed to close a 15-9 gap, bringing the score to 17-15. In the end, we didn’t win the match, but I proved my value to the coach.

In my six years of playing volleyball, I gained new friendships, focus, physical conditioning, mental health, and a lot of discipline—along with medals from championships and tournaments.

Basic rules

  • Team Composition: Each team has six players on the court (three in the front row and three in the back row).
  • Objective: The goal is to score points by sending the ball over the net and landing it in the opponent's court.
  • Scoring: A match is typically played best out of five sets. To win a set, a team must score 25 points (with a two-point advantage). The fifth set, if needed, is played to 15 points.
  • Serving: A player serves the ball from behind the end line and must get it over the net into the opponent’s court. The server continues until their team loses a rally.
  • Rotation: Teams rotate clockwise after winning a point on the opponent’s serve.
  • Rallies: Teams are allowed up to three touches (pass, set, attack) to return the ball to the opponent. A player cannot hit the ball twice consecutively.
  • Net Violations: Players cannot touch the net or cross the center line under the net during play.
  • Ball In/Out: The ball must land within the boundaries of the court. If it lands outside, the opposing team earns the point.
  • Blocking and Attacking: Front-row players can block or spike the ball near the net. Back-row players may attack only from behind the 10-foot line (3-meter line).
  • Substitutions: Teams can make a limited number of substitutions during a set, typically up to six.
